Why this mattersThe institution can file once. You can file too.

An institutional response is already underway, and it needs your individual voice next to it.

Under the Administrative Procedure Act, OMB has to read and respond to every substantive comment, and a high volume of them builds the record that supports a congressional stay and any later challenge. Even if OMB does not change course, that record is not wasted: the comments become the evidence base for the legal challenges likely to follow, and the courts may be where this is ultimately decided. The rule would put senior political appointees over every discretionary grant, make peer review advisory, allow active awards to be cancelled for shifting priorities, and restrict publication, conferences, and international collaboration. It binds every federal science agency on October 1, 2026. A flooded, substantive docket is the highest-leverage, lowest-risk thing almost anyone can do, and it closes in weeks.

How to write one that countsFour moves, and one rule about your name

  1. Pick a provision and name it. Cite the section number, for example §200.205. A short reference list is below to help you choose.
  2. Say what it does to real work. Use a concrete example from your own research or program. Specifics carry far more weight than generalities.
  3. Propose an alternative if you can. What would work better, and why. Reviewers read for fixes, not just objections.
  4. Keep it yours. Do not copy and paste shared text. Identical comments are counted once, so personalize every submission.

Your comment does not need to be long, formal, or polished. A clear letter about one concrete harm carries real weight, and comments can come from anyone, not only organizations.

One comment per institution OMB counts only one comment per institution. If you file as an individual, describe yourself generically, for example "a faculty member in [field] at a large public university," and do not name your specific university. Personalize the substance and your example, not your affiliation. Anonymous comments are allowed, individuals and organizations can both comment, and comments from red districts and states carry extra weight.

Page charges, article processing charges, and open-access fees presumptively unallowable.

Your representativesFind your representatives

Beyond the public comment, your elected representatives are another point of contact on this rule. This section helps you find out who represents you and how to reach their offices. What you do with that information is up to you, and any message you send is your own, as an individual constituent.

Some background. A public comment asks OMB to reconsider its own rule. Congress can also act, by adding language to the spending bills that fund the government. Constituents writing about docket OMB-2026-0034 have been asking their members for that blocking language and telling OMB the changes to the federal grant rules (2 C.F.R. Part 200) are unacceptable.

An example of what a constituent message can look like "Hi, I'm a constituent in [your city or ZIP]. I'm asking [your representative or senator] to help stop the OMB rule that rewrites the federal grant rules, docket OMB-2026-0034. Please add blocking language to the appropriations bills so it cannot take effect, and tell OMB the changes are unacceptable. This rule threatens [our research, our schools, my child's opportunities], and once the damage is done, it cannot be undone. Thank you."

How to find them. Find your U.S. Representative at house.gov and your two U.S. Senators at senate.gov. The U.S. Capitol switchboard is (202) 224-3121.
